# trades.md — Trades Vertical Template
## Plumber · Electrician · Locksmith · Heating · Handyman

**Applies to:** Trades clients across product types 1–3 (per `TECH.md` §1). Most local trades land at Type 1 (info + phone CTA) or Type 2 (info + quote-request form).

**Reference audit:** 2026-05-13. Three sites analyzed at 1280 / 768 / 375 viewports — Pimlico Plumbers (UK), Roto-Rooter (US), Banham (UK). Full per-site notes in §9.

**Trades is the hardest vertical to template** because most local trade businesses have no real web presence and the strong references are all chains or premium-craft brands. The agency's bread-and-butter trades client (single-operator electrician, plumber, locksmith) needs a fourth, simpler archetype that isn't on any reference site list — included below as Archetype D.

**Use this doc as a moodboard with principles, not a layout to clone.** A single-operator electrician should NOT copy Pimlico's three-popup-and-fleet hero — that pattern only works at scale. Templates are guidance, not assignments.

---

## Rules at a glance

- **The phone is the conversion.** Trades buyers want a human ASAP. Phone number must be the most prominent element above the fold; postcode/area search is the second most prominent.
- **Pick the right archetype to the client's scale.** Single-operator → D. Multi-location chain → B. Long-history premium → A. Volume emergency service → B.
- **Trust signals are everything.** Trades is a high-trust low-vanity vertical. License #, years in business, vehicle photo, real face of the owner, certifications — every single one matters more than design polish.
- **Service-area is a real concern.** "Do you cover my postcode?" is the user's first thought. Postcode search / service-area map / explicit borough list above the fold.
- **Emergency framing wins** for plumbers / electricians / locksmiths. "Open 24/7" / "Same-day service" / "Average response time: 47 minutes" — quantified urgency converts.
- **Real photos of the team in branded vehicles** is the single highest-leverage trust photography for trades. Stock-photo trades photography is the loudest tell in the vertical.
- **Pricing transparency or pricing reassurance** — either publish call-out rates ("£89 + parts for diagnostic visit") or state "Free quote, no obligation." Hiding pricing in trades costs leads.

## 1. The four trades archetypes

Trades has **four** rather than three archetypes because the agency's bread-and-butter client (single-operator) doesn't map to any of the high-end reference sites; it needs its own pattern.

| Archetype | Brand priority | Primary CTA | Production cost |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **A. Heritage Craft Premium** | Long history, craftsmanship, premium positioning | Enquire / Visit showroom | High — requires architectural / craft-object photography + premium typography |
| **B. Conversion Chain Emergency** | Fleet scale, 24/7 availability, multi-area coverage | Call now / Schedule online | Medium-high — needs fleet photography, dispatch system, multiple service categories |
| **C. Insurance-Backed Network** (covered briefly — not the agency's typical client) | Marketplace trust, vetted operators, single contact point | Find a tradesperson | Out of agency scope; usually national platforms |
| **D. Local Single-Operator Trust-Led** | The owner's face, certifications, area covered, phone | Call / WhatsApp | Low — the agency's default for most Berlin trades clients |

**Default for new local trades clients:** Archetype D (stripped down). Use B only if the client has 2+ vans / 5+ employees. Use A only if the client has a 30+ year history and a story worth telling.

## 2. Information architecture per archetype

### Archetype A — Heritage Craft Premium (Banham)

1. **Newsletter / story modal** — heritage signal opener ("Handcrafted In Britain Since 1926" / "Subscribe for craft stories")
2. **Hero** — restrained serif headline anchored to year ("Since 1926") + architectural / craft-object photography
3. **Featured product categories** — multi-card grid with named objects (Signature Locks, Bespoke Doors, etc.). Photography is the content.
4. **Brand showcase / made-by-brands** — sister brands or notable partnerships
5. **At your service / craftsmanship tabs** — black-and-white archive imagery of the craft
6. **Trust block** — recent press, reviews, awards
7. **Featured people / craftspeople** — real portraits of the makers
8. **Newsletter signup** — repeated below
9. **Footer** — large multi-column with sub-brand directory

### Archetype B — Conversion Chain Emergency (Pimlico, Roto-Rooter)

1. **Top utility bar** — phone number HUGE in accent color, opening hours, area covered
2. **Service-category nav row** — Plumbing / Electrical / Heating / etc. as tabbed nav
3. **Hero** — headline ("London's No.1 trusted name") + postcode/zip search prominent + fleet photo with branded vehicle + uniformed operator
4. **Promotional ribbon** — limited-time offer ("Get £25 off your order today") — coupon visual
5. **Service-category cards** — 4-6 cards (Plumbing / Drains / Water Damage / Water Heaters / Emergency / Heating) each with photo + name + CTA
6. **"Leave it to the pros" trust section** — bulleted credentials (Reputation, Transparency, Efficiency, Quality, Financing)
7. **Service-area coverage** — list of boroughs / metro areas covered + price guide if applicable
8. **Reviews carousel** — three Google reviews with star ratings
9. **FAQ / video content**
10. **Recruitment block** — "Plumbers Wanted" — signals scale
11. **Tagline + newsletter signup**
12. **Footer** — corporate-style multi-column

### Archetype C — Insurance-Backed Network (mentioned for completeness, rarely an agency client)

This archetype is for national platforms (Angi, Thumbtack, GetNinjas, MyBuilder, Checkatrade). They're aggregators, not operators. Reference UX only — never build this for a small business.

### Archetype D — Local Single-Operator Trust-Led (the agency's default trades client)

The vast majority of trades clients we'll get in Berlin / Lisbon / Brazil look like this. The IA is intentionally simple.

1. **Top bar** — phone number + WhatsApp link as the only chrome
2. **Hero** — headline naming the operator + the service + the area ("João — Eletricista em Cedofeita") + portrait of the operator in working clothes + ONE primary CTA (Call / WhatsApp)
3. **Service list** — 5–8 bullets ("Reparações elétricas", "Instalações", "Quadros elétricos", etc.) — not cards, just a list
4. **Service area** — text statement ("Cobertura: Porto e arredores até 15 km") — no map iframe required
5. **Trust block** — license number visible (CAE or local equivalent), years in business, 1–3 Google review pull-quotes
6. **Pricing reassurance** — single statement ("Orçamento gratuito sem compromisso") or call-out rate if applicable
7. **Visit / Contact block** — phone + WhatsApp + email + working hours
8. **Footer** — minimal, legal info (NIF), social if any

No service-category cards. No fleet photography (the operator IS the fleet). No emergency 24/7 framing unless the operator actually offers it. Most simple operators land at 5 sections total.

## 3. Hero patterns

### Archetype A — Heritage anchor

- **Year of founding** as the dominant trust signal ("Since 1926" / "Handcrafted in Britain Since 1926")
- **Restrained serif headline** — not shouty, not promotional
- **Architectural / craft-object photography** — close-up on the work product (a brass lock, a painted door, a hand-forged key)
- **No prominent phone number** in hero — the brand is premium enough to invite the user to write an enquiry instead
- **No promotional banner** — discounts cheapen the heritage frame

### Archetype B — Conversion / urgency

- **Phone number ENORMOUS** — usually red or yellow, in the top utility bar at full font weight. Roto-Rooter shows "Call Us Now! 800-768-6911" in 60+ pt
- **Postcode / zip search** as the second-most-prominent element ("Find Your Local Plumber" + zip code input)
- **Headline with trust + scale** — "The plumbing experts you've trusted for over 90 years" / "London's No.1 trusted name"
- **Fleet photo + uniformed operator** — branded van + worker in uniform standing nearby. The combination signals: "real company, real people, branded vehicle, comes to you"
- **Promotional ribbon** below hero — limited-time offer with explicit terms ("Get £25 off / FAST, FRIENDLY AND PROFESSIONAL SERVICE")
- **Service-category nav** above hero — tabs/links for the different services offered

### Archetype D — Single-operator hero

- **Headline = operator + service + area** ("João — Eletricista em Cedofeita / Sasha — Plumber in Kreuzberg")
- **Portrait of the operator** in working clothes — real photo, in a real setting. Not in front of a generic background.
- **ONE CTA in the brand color** — "Ligar agora" or WhatsApp button
- **Phone number visible as text below CTA** — for users who prefer to dial directly
- **License number small but visible** ("CAE 4321" or "Master Electrician License #1234")
- **Area covered as a single line** ("Cobre Porto até 15 km / Berlin: Mitte, Kreuzberg, Friedrichshain")

The single-operator hero is QUIET, not LOUD. The brand isn't the spectacle; the operator is the trust.

## 4. Photography direction

### Universal trades rules

- **The subject is real workmanship or real people.** Not stock plumbers crossing arms, not stock electricians smiling at sockets.
- **Branded vehicle in the frame** is high-leverage when authentic. A photo of the actual van with the company logo signals "real operation, comes to me."
- **Tools visible** is acceptable — but only the operator's actual tools, not stock-photo tool sets.
- **Real face of the owner / lead operator** in a photo somewhere on the site. Single-operator businesses NEED this; chains can skip it.
- **Architectural close-up** (Banham archetype) for premium / craft trades — shows the work product (lock, door, fixture, finish) with magazine-quality lighting.

### Per-archetype photography notes

| Archetype | Photo style |
|-----------|------------|
| A — Heritage Craft | Architectural close-up · black-and-white archive shots · craft-object hero · selective color · museum-quality |
| B — Conversion Chain | Branded van + uniformed operator · bright daylight · multiple service-category photos · stylized but accessible |
| D — Single Operator | One real portrait of the owner (working clothes, real context — outside the van, on a job site, in the workshop). Smile optional but not faked. |

## 5. Typography pairings

### What works in trades

Trades has **two competing typography traditions**:

- **Utilitarian sans-serif** — Conversion-Chain archetype. Bold, no-nonsense, often combined with red/yellow accent colors. Inter or Söhne work; even Arial works at this archetype if the colors are right.
- **Editorial serif** — Heritage Craft archetype. Restrained, magazine-quality, italic emphasis on key words ("Since 1926").

**Tier 1 pairings:**

| Display | Body | Archetype fit |
|---------|------|---------------|
| **Inter / Söhne** (bold uppercase tracked) | **Inter / Söhne** | B — utilitarian conversion |
| **Fraunces / Domaine Display** (restrained roman) | **Söhne / Inter** | A — heritage craft premium |
| **Fraunces** (regular weight, no italic) | **Inter / Manrope** | D — single operator (warm + trustworthy, not shouty) |

**Operating rules:**
- **Avoid italic for trades** unless going full heritage (Archetype A). Italic for "Same day service" reads as decorative and weakens the urgency signal.
- **No cursive script anywhere in trades.** Trades clients want competence signals, not elegance signals.
- **Phone numbers must use a font with strong tabular figures.** Wonky-aligned digits in a phone number screams unprofessional. Use `font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums` and a font with real tabular numerals.

## 6. Color archetypes

### Archetype A — Heritage Craft Premium

| Direction | Palette | Mood |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Navy + brass** | Deep navy bg / cream text / brass accent | Banham — premium heritage British craft |
| **Charcoal + copper** | Charcoal bg / bone text / warm copper accent | Modern heritage, less British, more European |
| **Forest + stone** | Forest green bg / sand text / stone accent | Botanical / land-rooted craft |

### Archetype B — Conversion Chain Emergency

| Direction | Palette | Mood |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Red + navy** | White bg / navy primary / RED CTAs / yellow promotional accents | Pimlico — UK patriotic urgency |
| **Blue + red** | White bg / dark blue primary / red phone-CTA accent | Roto-Rooter — American utilitarian |
| **Yellow + black** | White bg / black text / bright YELLOW CTAs | Trade hazard / "we mean business" |
| **Green + white** | White bg / forest green primary / green CTAs | Eco / sustainability angle for newer green-trades brands |

### Archetype D — Single-Operator Trust-Led

| Direction | Palette | Mood |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Warm earth** | Cream bg / dark brown text / orange or terracotta accent | Warm, approachable, "neighbour you can trust" |
| **Clean blue** | White bg / dark navy text / mid-blue accent | Clean, professional, no-nonsense |
| **Soft green** | Cream bg / dark green text / sage accent | Reliable, calm, environmentally-conscious framing |

**Rules:**
- **Conversion-chain colors must include RED or YELLOW for the phone CTA.** Trades buyers expect emergency-color signaling on the call button.
- **Heritage colors avoid bright accents entirely.** Banham uses brass — saturated but warm. No bright red, no neon yellow.
- **Single-operator should choose ONE brand color** and use it sparingly. Trades sites that try three accent colors look amateur.

## 7. Copy voice cues

### What to say

- **The operator's name in the headline** for Archetype D. "João — Eletricista em Cedofeita" beats "Reliable Electrician Services in Porto."
- **Specific service language**, not generic ("Reparação de instalações elétricas" beats "All electrical needs")
- **Quantified trust** — "Since 1926" / "Over 90 years" / "Average response time: 47 minutes" / "200+ five-star reviews"
- **Service area named explicitly** — neighbourhood / Postleitzahl / borough list. Users need to know "do you cover me" before anything else.
- **License / certification number visible** — Master Plumber #4321 / CAE 12345 / Master Electrician License — provides defensible credibility
- **Pricing reassurance** — either rate ("£89 + parts call-out fee") or commitment ("Orçamento gratuito, sem compromisso")
- **24/7 / emergency framing** when true — "Always available for emergencies — call any time" — for plumbers and locksmiths especially

### What never to say

- "Your trusted local plumber" (says nothing specific)
- "We do it all" (vague, smells of one-man-band)
- "Quality you can trust" (boilerplate; replace with the actual credential)
- "Quick response time" without a number — say "average 47 minutes" or omit
- "Family-run since [year]" if it's not literally true
- "No job too big or too small" (template phrase across thousands of trades sites)
- Generic "Welcome to [Trade Business]"

## 8. Trades-specific anti-patterns

Beyond the generic anti-slop in `DESIGN-BEST-PRACTICES.md`:

| Anti-pattern | Why it's a tell | Fix |
|--------------|----------------|-----|
| **Stock photo plumber crossing arms in front of pipe** | The single most overused stock image in trades. Indistinguishable across 10,000 sites. | Real photo of the actual operator. Even a phone photo of them next to their van beats stock. |
| **"Stock fleet" of generic blue vans** | Photoshopped van fleet that's clearly not the client's actual vehicles | Real photo of the actual van, even if it's just one. Park it in the actual street if possible. |
| **"Award-winning service"** without naming the award | Empty trust claim | Name it (Checkatrade Approved Trader 2023, GasSafe Registered, Master Plumber Diploma) or omit |
| **3-card "Our Process" with generic icons** ("Call us → We arrive → Job done") | Filler content. Everyone has the same three steps. | Either be specific ("Diagnostic visit £89, written quote within 24 hours, work starts next day if accepted") or omit |
| **Fake testimonials with no source** ("Best plumber ever! — Sarah J.") | No Google attribution = inventable | Pull from Google Reviews with author + date visible + link to source. If none exist, omit the testimonial section entirely. |
| **"Call now" with no actual phone number visible** | Defeats the entire purpose | Phone number large, clickable `tel:`, in the chrome header sticky on scroll |
| **Hidden pricing with no rate signal** | Costs leads — trades buyers comparison-shop on price | Publish call-out rate OR explicit "Free quotes, no obligation" |
| **Map iframe of the entire UK / Germany / Brazil** suggesting nationwide coverage | Single-operator can't cover a country | List actual neighbourhoods / postcodes covered, no map |
| **"We're a family business"** without any names or faces | Generic and unverifiable | Photo + name + role of the actual operator(s) |
| **Generic "Welcome to [Trade Business]"** as h1 | Same problem as every other vertical | Replace with name + service + area |
| **Multiple service-category cards with placeholder photos** for services the operator doesn't really offer | Common when copying a chain template | List only the services the operator actually delivers. 3 specific services beats 12 vague ones. |
| **Booking calendar widget for emergency trades** (plumbing, locksmith) | Emergency callers will NOT use a booking widget | Phone CTA only for emergency services; booking only for non-urgent (heating maintenance, electrical inspection) |

## 9. Reference site annotations

### 9.1 Pimlico Plumbers — `pimlicoplumbers.com` (Archetype B)

**Audit date:** 2026-05-13. Captured at 1280 / 768 / 375.

**What makes it work:**

- **Phone number ENORMOUS in red** in the top-right of the header — visible from any scroll position
- **Service-category navigation row** in red — Plumbing / Electrical / etc. tabbed at the top so users find their service in 2 clicks
- **Postcode search prominent in hero** ("London's No.1 trusted name in plumbing" + postcode input). The user's first question ("do you cover me?") is the form's purpose.
- **Coupon "Get £25 off your order today"** — promotional layer; visible commitment of value
- **Fleet of red Pimlico vans visible** in the hero photography. The fleet IS the trust signal.
- **"24/7" / "Same day" service framing** throughout
- **Long-scrolling page** with multiple service cards, FAQ, video content, reviews — sells everything in one scroll
- **"Sign up to our blog" newsletter** at the footer — content marketing as a separate channel

**What we'd borrow:**

- The phone-in-the-header-bar pattern for any Archetype B trades client
- The postcode-area search as a hero element
- The service-category tabbed navigation when client has 3+ service lines

**What we wouldn't:**

- The full long-scroll with 12 sections — that's chain-scale content. Single-operator client gets 5–6 sections max.

### 9.2 Roto-Rooter — `rotorooter.com` (Archetype B)

**Audit date:** 2026-05-13. Captured at 1280 / 768 / 375.

**What makes it work:**

- **"Call Us Now! 800-768-6911"** as a hero element rendered at 36–48px size in red. No subtlety — it's a phone CTA.
- **Zip code search** as the secondary hero element ("Find Your Local Plumber")
- **Branded van + plumber in uniform standing next to it** — the canonical trades-conversion photo
- **"OVER 90 YEARS" heritage signal** integrated into hero copy. Heritage flag without the heritage-Archetype-A restraint.
- **"FAST, FRIENDLY AND PROFESSIONAL SERVICE. SCHEDULE NOW!"** promotional ribbon below hero
- **4-card service grid** — Plumbing and Drains / Water Damage / Water Heaters / Emergency Plumber — equal-weight cards
- **"LEAVE IT TO THE PROS"** with bulleted credentials (Reputation, Transparency, Efficiency, Quality, Financing)
- **Three Google reviews** with star ratings — single quote per card, real-looking
- **"Plumbers Wanted!"** recruitment block — signals scale + ongoing growth
- **"And Away Go Troubles Down the Drain"** brand jingle/tagline at footer — iconic American advertising

**What we'd borrow:**

- The 4-card service grid pattern (each card photo + name + CTA — clean conversion design)
- The bulleted "credentials" section ("Reputation / Transparency / Efficiency...") — gives concrete trust without empty claims
- The "Plumbers Wanted!" recruitment block — surfaces scale without being self-congratulatory

**What we wouldn't:**

- The full corporate footer with multiple sub-brands and app store buttons — too much chrome for any agency-size client
- The brand jingle approach — works for a 90-year-old American brand, falls flat for a new Berlin operator

### 9.3 Banham — `banham.co.uk` (Archetype A)

**Audit date:** 2026-05-13. Captured at 1280 / 768 / 375.

**What makes it work:**

- **"Handcrafted in Britain Since 1926"** — the entire brand hangs on the year. 99 years of heritage signals craft you can't get from a 6-month-old startup.
- **Newsletter modal at top of page** opens with the heritage signal (similar to TONI&GUY in beauty). Captures email before scroll.
- **Architectural / craft-object photography** — brass door knockers, painted blue doors, ornate hardware. The work is photographed like jewelry.
- **No prominent phone number** — Banham doesn't compete on speed; they compete on quality. The user is invited to write a longer enquiry instead.
- **"Made by Brands"** showcase — Banham makes locks for branded products, signals B2B craft credibility
- **Tabbed "At Your Service"** with black-and-white archival imagery
- **"We are real..."** notebook-and-pen photo — signals literal craftsmanship + paper records
- **Featured craftspeople** with portraits — names + roles, real humans
- **"Trusted. Since 1926."** footer signature — closing tagline that re-affirms the brand promise

**What we'd borrow:**

- The "Since [year]" anchoring when the client has the years to back it
- The architectural / craft-object photography style for premium trades
- The restrained typography — no shouting, no urgency, just craft

**What we wouldn't:**

- The full magazine layout — too content-heavy for the typical agency client
- The "no phone in hero" approach for any trades client without 50+ years of brand equity

## 10. Decision matrix — picking the archetype per client

| Question | If yes → | If no → |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Is the client a single operator or 2-person team? | **Archetype D** (default for most agency trades clients) | Continue |
| Does the client have 30+ years of history with a brand story? | Archetype A — heritage craft | Continue |
| Does the client run a fleet of 5+ vans and offer 24/7 dispatch? | Archetype B — conversion chain | Continue |
| Is the client an emergency-response trade (plumber / locksmith / electrician)? | Archetype B if at scale; Archetype D with emergency framing if solo | — |
| Is the client a non-emergency trade (heating maintenance, gardening, painting)? | Archetype D with appointment-booking | — |
| Does the client want online booking (Calendly / TradeBox / similar)? | Type 2 product type, Archetype D with booking link | — |

**The agency's reality:** ~95 % of trades clients in Berlin / Lisbon / Brazil will be Archetype D (single operator). Build the muscle for D first; A and B are aspirational templates for chain/heritage clients who will be rare.
